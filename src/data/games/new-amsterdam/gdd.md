# 1. Game Overview

## 1.1 High-Concept Pitch

New Amsterdam is a single-player, open-world action-RPG set exclusively on the island of Manhattan. The player assumes the role of Dutch, an immortal vampire cursed to never leave the island, who witnesses and participates in New York City's entire history — from a Dutch colonial settlement in 1626 to the modern-day metropolis. History is not backdrop; it is the gameplay.

## 1.3 Elevator Pitch

> *"You cannot leave. You cannot die. You can only watch — and feed."*

A wealthy Dutch merchant sails to the New World in 1626 and is turned into a vampire before setting foot on land. Arriving in New Amsterdam — the settlement that will become New York City — he is immediately cursed by the Algonquin people who inhabit the island, binding him to Manhattan forever. Across centuries of real history, through colonial expansion, revolution, industrialization, prohibition, war, and the birth of modern New York, Dutch survives, adapts, feeds, and grows more powerful. The city is both his prison and his empire.

## 1.4 Design Pillars

| Pillar | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **PILLAR 1: Living History** | The world evolves in real time. Buildings rise and fall. Political figures walk the streets. Subway systems are built beneath the player's feet. History is not a cutscene — it is the world the player inhabits. |
| **PILLAR 2: Predator's Playground** | Movement is fast, fluid, and predatory. Climbing, leaping, and stalking through Manhattan should feel thrilling and dangerous. The city is Dutch's hunting ground. |
| **PILLAR 3: The Weight of Eternity** | Dutch's immortality is a burden. The player must manage relationships, reputation, hunger, and the emotional toll of watching everyone they know die. Power comes at a cost. |
| **PILLAR 4: Neighborhood Identity** | Each neighborhood of Manhattan has its own distinct culture, danger level, political climate, and opportunity. The world rewards players who truly know their neighborhood. |

# 2. Story & Narrative

## 2.1 The Origin Story (Prologue)

Year: 1626. Location: The Atlantic Ocean, aboard a Dutch merchant vessel.

A prosperous Dutch businessman — hereafter referred to as 'Dutch' — has sold his holdings in Europe and is sailing to the New World to establish his trade empire in the growing settlement of New Amsterdam on the southern tip of Manhattan Island.

Midway through the voyage, during the night watch, Dutch is attacked by a fellow passenger — a wild-eyed man who bites him savagely on the neck, then flings himself overboard into the black ocean before anyone can intervene. Dutch dismisses the wound and retires to his private cabin.

The next morning, sunlight through the porthole feels like fire on his skin. Food turns his stomach. A rat scurries across the floor of his cabin. Dutch smells something overwhelming — copper, warm, alive. He does not hesitate. He kills the rat and drinks.

Over the remaining days at sea, Dutch quietly drains every rat on the ship, then moves to the livestock stored below decks. By the time the vessel reaches New Amsterdam, the animals are dead and Dutch has begun to understand the terrible truth of what he has become.

## 2.2 Arrival — New Amsterdam, 1626

Dutch arrives to find a small but ambitious colony. The streets are muddy. The language is familiar. The darkness feels like home. Refusing to feed on humans, Dutch retreats to the wooded northern end of the island each night to hunt deer that roam freely there.

He is observed. The Lenape-speaking Algonquin people who have inhabited this island for centuries watch this night creature that does not sleep, does not eat, and moves through the forest like a predator without equal. They call a council. They believe he is a demon born of the dark water.

## 2.3 The Ambush & The First Kill

A group of Algonquin warriors set a trap in the northern woods. They spring it while Dutch is hunting, surrounding him with fire and noise. In the chaos of the struggle, one warrior's blade catches Dutch's lip. Blood hits his tongue.

Human blood. For the first time.

Something breaks loose inside him. The restraint he has carefully maintained collapses in an instant. Dutch attacks with a ferocity that horrifies even him afterward. When the red fades from his vision, every warrior is dead. Dutch stands in the forest, trembling, blood-soaked, devastated.

He is not just changed. He is something new and something terrible. He spends the next week in the woods, refusing to return to the settlement, bargaining with himself, setting rules he is afraid he cannot keep.

## 2.4 The Curse

The Algonquin elders, understanding that they cannot kill the demon, summon something older. A binding ritual — part prayer, part poison, part geography — ties Dutch to the island itself. He feels it like a collar tightening around his throat when he approaches the water. He attempts to board a vessel. He cannot step aboard. The compulsion to remain is absolute and physical, like fighting gravity.

He is trapped. Manhattan is his cage. It will also become, over the centuries, his kingdom.

Crucially, the curse has a secondary effect the elders may not have fully understood: no other vampire can set foot on the island either. The barrier that imprisons Dutch also protects the island from the rest of the vampire world. For centuries, Dutch is completely alone in his kind.

## 2.5 The Century Act Structure

| Act | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| **Act 0 — Prologue (1626)** | The voyage, the bite, the transformation. Tutorial systems introduced. Dutch arrives broken and confused. |
| **Act 1 — The Colonial Era (1626–1776)** | New Amsterdam becomes New York under British rule. Dutch establishes his first underground residence, begins building wealth, and learns to survive invisibly among humans. |
| **Act 2 — Revolution (1776–1800)** | The Revolutionary War tears through New York. British occupation. Fire. Chaos. Dutch must choose sides — or exploit both. Washington and his officers walk these streets. |
| **Act 3 — The Gilded City (1800–1920)** | Industrialization, immigration, the rise of Tammany Hall, the building of the Brooklyn Bridge, the subway system carved beneath Dutch's feet. The city explodes upward and inward. Wealth and organized crime bloom simultaneously. |
| **Act 4 — Prohibition & Shadow (1920–1950)** | Bootleggers, jazz clubs, speakeasies. The mob rules New York. Dutch, centuries old and immensely wealthy, becomes a silent power behind criminal empires. World War II rattles the city. The island fills with soldiers shipping out — and never returning. |
| **Act 5 — The Modern Era (1950–Present)** | The city Dutch built around himself is unrecognizable and yet completely familiar. For the first time since 1626, he is not alone. The airplane has arrived. The barrier that kept vampires away cannot stop those who fly over it. Dutch meets his first vampire — and discovers that the immortal world has been watching Manhattan for centuries. |

## 2.6 The Vampire Arrival — Act 5 Story Beat

The introduction of other vampires in Act 5 is the game's largest narrative turning point. Dutch has defined himself in isolation — his ethics, his rules, his hunting methods, his relationship with the city — all built without reference to any other of his kind.

The arriving vampires are not what Dutch expects. Some are ancient. Some are freshly turned. Some are horrified by what Dutch has done with his centuries of solitude — amassed power and wealth and community connections in ways vampires are not supposed to. Others are there to claim a piece of his empire.

The curse remains tied to Dutch specifically. He still cannot leave. But others can come and go freely. This creates an entirely new power dynamic that drives the endgame narrative.

| Attribute | Detail |

# 3. World & Setting

## 3.1 The Island as a Character

Manhattan is not just a setting. It is the game's central character, second only to Dutch himself. The island is Dutch's prison, his home, his hunting ground, his investment portfolio, and his legacy. The player should feel the claustrophobic enormity of being trapped somewhere that simultaneously keeps expanding and transforming around them.

## 3.2 The Living City System

New Amsterdam features a proprietary Living City System in which the world physically and socially evolves as Act milestones are reached. This is one of the most technically ambitious aspects of the game and a core design pillar.

- Buildings are constructed in historically accurate sequences — players may watch the Empire State Building rise steel girder by steel girder across multiple in-game sessions.
- Streets change from dirt paths to cobblestone to paved roads to modern asphalt.
- The subway system is carved into the earth — sections of underground tunnel open as new Act milestones are reached.
- Population density increases each Act. The streets of 1626 are near-empty. Modern Manhattan is a living sea of humans.
- Technology evolves: gas lamps replace torches, electric streetlights replace gas lamps, neon signs appear, and eventually screens and digital displays fill the skyline.
- Weather, seasons, and holidays play out in real time. The city decorates for Christmas, goes dark during wartime blackouts, celebrates V-Day with crowds in the streets.

## 3.3 Neighborhood Design Philosophy

Manhattan is divided into distinct neighborhoods, each with its own identity, danger profile, political climate, and available activities. The design philosophy is to make each neighborhood feel genuinely different to inhabit — not just visually, but mechanically and socially.

| Neighborhood | Identity |
|-------------|----------|
| **Lower Manhattan** | Dutch's original territory. The oldest architecture survives here longer. The financial district grows around the remnants of the original colonial settlement. Dutch has deep roots and deep reputation here. |
| **Five Points / Lower East Side** | The immigrant quarter across the centuries. Irish, Italian, Jewish, Chinese — waves of new arrivals each bringing new culture, new food smells Dutch cannot eat, new music he learns to love. High crime, high density, high opportunity. |
| **Midtown** | The modern power center. Skyscrapers replace brownstones. Corporate empires occupy buildings Dutch owns the foundations of. By Act 4-5, this is where political and business deals are made. |
| **Harlem** | Jazz. The Harlem Renaissance. Community so vibrant that Dutch — the outsider, the eternal observer — is drawn to it despite himself. Some of his most meaningful human connections across the centuries happen here. |
| **Upper West / East Side** | Old money. Society. The neighborhoods where Dutch's mortal identity (maintained through a series of fabricated identities and legal structures) is most respectable and most fragile. |
| **Northern Tip / Inwood** | Where it all began. The forest where the Algonquin trapped him. As the city creeps northward, Dutch watches this land change from wilderness to park. The curse anchor point is here. He can feel it. |

## 3.4 Dutch's Properties

A core progression system involves Dutch acquiring real estate across Manhattan over the centuries. Properties serve multiple gameplay functions:

- Safe daylight refuge — Dutch needs a place to shelter before sunrise. Being caught in the open at dawn is a fail state.
- Storage and upgrade hubs — weapons, disguises, historical documents, blood stores.
- Income generation — properties earn passive in-game currency across time.
- Historical anchors — each property holds memories. Visiting an old property unlocks narrative vignettes showing what happened there decades prior.

## 3.5 The Barrier

The Algonquin curse manifests as an invisible barrier at the island's edges — at the water's boundary. Dutch experiences this as an overwhelming physical compulsion to stop, accompanied by visual distortion and a low, resonant audio cue that players learn to recognize as the curse making itself known.

Early game: the barrier is absolute. Dutch cannot cross the water under any circumstances.

Late game: Act 5 introduces the discovery that the barrier is specifically tied to Dutch — other vampires who fly or travel by air can bypass it entirely. This creates a new category of vulnerability Dutch has never experienced. He is no longer the only predator on the island.

# 4. Gameplay & Mechanics

## 4.1 Core Gameplay Loop

The core loop of New Amsterdam operates at three levels simultaneously:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Moment-to-Moment** | Hunt, feed, move, survive, avoid daylight. The immediate physical experience of being Dutch — fast, predatory, and operating on a nocturnal clock. |
| **Session-Level** | Complete story missions that advance historical eras, acquire properties, manage reputation in specific neighborhoods, and handle threats from hunters, rivals, or political enemies. |
| **Long-Term** | Build Dutch's empire across centuries. Accumulate power, wealth, and influence. Make permanent choices about Dutch's identity — how much of his humanity does he preserve? |

## 4.2 Movement System

Movement is the game's most immediately satisfying mechanic and the one most directly inspired by Assassin's Creed's parkour DNA — but adapted specifically for a vampire's supernatural capability.

- **Wall-climbing**: Dutch can scale virtually any vertical surface. Speed is significantly faster than a human climber — it reads as predatory, not athletic.
- **Leaping**: Dutch can leap extraordinary horizontal and vertical distances, especially from height. This makes rooftop traversal the fastest and most enjoyable method of moving through the city.
- **Ground speed**: Dutch runs faster than any human but movement on ground level is navigated through crowds, wagons, vehicles — the street-level texture of each era.
- **Evolution**: The movement system must evolve with the architecture. In 1626, Dutch climbs timber-framed colonial buildings. By Act 5, he is scaling glass-and-steel skyscrapers. Each era requires its own movement language.
- **Shadow system**: Moving through shadow or darkness grants Dutch a speed boost and reduces his visibility to NPCs. Players are rewarded for moving like a vampire — using darkness, elevation, and speed.

## 4.3 The Hunger System

Hunger is Dutch's most fundamental resource and the source of the game's core ethical tension.

| Mechanic | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| **Hunger Meter** | A persistent bar that depletes over time and increases with physical exertion (combat, movement). At low levels, Dutch's abilities weaken. At critical levels, control becomes unreliable. |
| **Feeding Options** | Animals (common, low satisfaction, widely available), Criminal NPCs (flagged targets who reduce reputation penalty), Willing sources (rare, later in game, expensive), Unwilling civilians (high satisfaction, significant moral/reputation cost). |
| **The Discipline Check** | When Hunger reaches critical levels, the player is presented with a temptation event — a vulnerable human nearby. Resisting costs a resource. Failing costs reputation and triggers a Dutch emotional response scene. |
| **Blood Quality** | Different blood sources provide different buffs. The blood of a powerful or healthy individual grants greater benefits. This creates a temptation to feed on specific targets rather than animals. |
| **Blood Storage** | Dutch can collect blood in stored containers (evolution: animal bladders in 1626 to medical-grade refrigerated storage in Act 5) kept at his properties, allowing feeding without hunting. |

## 4.4 Combat System

Combat in New Amsterdam is fast, brutal, and carries consequence. Dutch is enormously powerful compared to any human — but he is not reckless. Every fight risks exposure, reputation damage, and discovery.

- Dutch's supernatural strength means most human enemies are dispatched quickly. Combat against humans is designed to feel overwhelming from Dutch's perspective — the danger is not losing, it is being seen.
- Vampire hunters are the exception. They are equipped with specific knowledge and tools (silver, fire, sunlight-based weapons) that genuinely threaten Dutch. Hunter encounters are significantly more challenging.
- Stealth-kill system rewards silent feeding. A clean stealth kill leaves no witnesses and no reputation damage.
- Combat style evolves with the era — Dutch fights differently with a 17th-century rapier than with modern improvised weapons or his bare hands at full power.
- The 'Unleash' mechanic: at full hunger, Dutch can enter a brief, extremely powerful combat state — but doing so in public has severe reputation and narrative consequences.

## 4.5 The Identity System

Dutch cannot age. This means he cannot maintain a single public identity for more than a generation without arousing suspicion. The Identity System manages Dutch's mortal face to the world.

- Every 20-30 years of game time, Dutch must retire his current identity and construct a new one — new name, new background story, new social class entry point.
- Managing a credible identity requires actions: acquiring documentation, establishing witnesses who know the 'new' Dutch, eliminating anyone who knows he is the same man from decades ago.
- Failed identity management triggers Hunter attention — secret societies who have tracked Dutch across centuries appear as antagonists.
- Successful identity chains unlock Legacy Bonuses — social capital, inherited wealth structures, property chains that survived the identity transition.

## 4.6 The Reputation System

Dutch operates across multiple overlapping reputation tracks, each corresponding to different factions across each historical era:

| Track | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Street Reputation** | How the common people of each neighborhood perceive Dutch's current identity. High street rep = safe passage, tips, protection. Low rep = hostility, reporting to authorities. |
| **Power Reputation** | How political figures, crime bosses, and institutional power perceive Dutch. This unlocks mission types and alliance opportunities unavailable to low-rep players. |
| **Hunter Awareness** | A hidden track measuring how much vampire-hunting organizations know about Dutch specifically. High awareness triggers active Hunter missions — assassins sent specifically for Dutch. |
| **Historical Legacy** | A long-term track measuring what Dutch's various identities have contributed to the city. High legacy unlocks special late-game narrative outcomes and unique properties. |

## 4.7 Seasonal & Historical Events

The Living City System generates real historical and seasonal events that the player can engage with, ignore, or exploit:

- **Seasonal**: Winter brings longer nights (more hunting time), holiday crowds (more cover in public), and harder animal hunting. Summer brings shorter nights (more dangerous), more street activity.
- **Historical**: Major events — the British takeover of New Amsterdam (1664), the Great Fire of New York (1776), the Draft Riots (1863), Prohibition (1920), the 1977 blackout, 9/11 — all occur and dramatically reshape the city and Dutch's operational environment.
- **Holidays**: The city dresses differently. NPC behavior changes. Crowds gather. Some events provide unique feeding opportunities or unique cover. Others create dangerous exposure.
- **Player choice**: Dutch can participate in historical events (affecting outcomes within the game world), observe from a distance, or use the distraction to pursue his own agenda.

## 4.8 The Familiar System

Dutch cannot walk in the sun, but his empire never sleeps. To manage his daytime affairs, Dutch binds a human Familiar to his service. This Familiar is a fully playable character during the daylight hours, handling business transactions, scouting new properties, gathering intel, and standing guard over Dutch's vulnerable slumber. Unlike Dutch, the Familiar relies entirely on human combat skills, stealth, and period-specific weapons—bringing a completely different, grounded gameplay style to daytime sections. Since Familiars age and eventually die, the player must carefully recruit and train replacements across the centuries, losing a loyal friend only to find another.

# 5. Progression & Powers

## 5.1 Vampire Power Progression

Dutch grows more powerful over the centuries. Power progression is tied to story milestones (Acts completed) and to choices the player makes about how Dutch feeds and what he prioritizes. Power cannot be rushed — it accumulates with age.

| Act | Powers |
|-----|--------|
| **Act 1 (1626–1776)** | Base vampire abilities: enhanced strength, speed, wall-climbing, night vision. Hunger meter is short and punishing. Dutch is powerful compared to humans but vulnerable and uncertain. |
| **Act 2 (1776–1800)** | Mental abilities begin to emerge: basic mesmerism (short-duration suggestion on single targets). Hunger meter grows. Dutch is more controlled. |
| **Act 3 (1800–1920)** | Full mesmerism (crowd suggestion, sustained mental influence). Enhanced healing. Ability to move through shallow water briefly. Speed and climbing reach their peak human-observable range. |
| **Act 4 (1920–1950)** | Supernatural speed becomes difficult for humans to track. Blood sense develops — Dutch can detect nearby humans by heartbeat through walls. Advanced stealth abilities. |
| **Act 5 (1950–Present)** | Dutch at full ancient power. Abilities that exceed normal vampire capabilities, developed through centuries of solitude and discipline. Specific endgame powers TBD through iteration. |

## 5.2 Wealth & Empire Progression

Dutch's economic empire is a parallel progression system that grows across the Acts. Starting from a modest stake of capital he arrived with in 1626, Dutch leverages his immortality's greatest practical advantage: compound interest across centuries.

- **Early Acts**: Dutch must carefully manage modest resources. Acquiring his first property is a major milestone.
- **Middle Acts**: Dutch's earliest investments have had decades to compound. He begins acquiring multiple properties and can fund human proxies to manage business interests.
- **Late Acts**: Dutch is, by any reasonable measure, extraordinarily wealthy. He operates through layers of shell identities and corporate structures. Manhattan real estate he bought for nothing in the 1700s is worth billions.
- **Wealth unlocks**: access to restricted areas, ability to bribe officials, hiring specialized human agents, acquiring rare equipment, and funding the construction or renovation of specific properties.

# 6. Characters & Factions

## 6.1 Recurring Character Types

Because Dutch outlives everyone he meets, New Amsterdam uses a recurring archetype system rather than persistent named companions. Each era introduces characters who fill similar emotional and functional roles — but they are always new people, always mortal, always temporary.

| Archetype | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **The Confidant** | One person per era who knows Dutch's true nature (or suspects it) and chooses to help rather than hunt. These relationships are the emotional heart of each Act. Their deaths at the end of each era are significant narrative moments. |
| **The Hunter** | Each era has a vampire-hunting tradition that targets Dutch. Sometimes religious. Sometimes governmental. Sometimes a private society. Each iteration is more sophisticated than the last. |
| **The Political Ally** | A powerful human figure whose relationship with Dutch's current identity provides access and protection. Politicians, crime bosses, military officers — the specific flavor changes with the era. |
| **The Threat** | A human or supernatural force that directly endangers Dutch's existence or empire in each Act. The nature of the threat evolves. |

## 6.2 Historical Figures

Dutch will interact with real historical figures throughout the game. These interactions are designed with historical accuracy as a foundation, with fictional liberties taken only where necessary for gameplay.

- **Peter Stuyvesant** — the famously stubborn Dutch colonial governor. Dutch would have known him.
- **Alexander Hamilton** — young, ambitious, operating in exactly the social circles Dutch's Act 2 identity would inhabit.
- **Boss Tweed** — the Tammany Hall political machine is a perfect vehicle for Dutch's Act 3 power-building.
- **Prohibition-era mob figures** — Lucky Luciano, Arnold Rothstein and others operated in spaces Dutch would inhabit in Act 4.

*Note: All historical figure interactions will be reviewed for historical sensitivity and accuracy during development.*

## 6.3 Factions

| Faction | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **The Algonquin Legacy** | Descendants of the tribe that cursed Dutch. They have not forgotten. Some are allies who understand his nature. Some seek to lift or tighten the curse. A thread that runs through the entire game. |
| **The Order of the Pale** | A vampire-hunting secret society that has tracked Dutch across centuries. They are more sophisticated and better-funded in each Act. By Act 5, they are a global organization. |
| **The Criminal Underworld** | Shifts with each era — pirates, political machines, bootleggers, the Mafia. Dutch's wealth and longevity make him a natural silent partner to whoever holds street power. |
| **The Witches** | Hidden covens operating in the shadows of New York. Witches have their own ancient prerogatives and magics. They are unpredictable—sometimes forging fragile alliances with Dutch, other times acting as his most dangerous, esoteric foes. |
| **The Arriving Vampires** | Act 5 faction. Other immortals who have been watching Manhattan from a distance. Because of his isolation and age, Dutch is vastly more powerful in raw strength than these newcomers. However, these vampires have spent centuries learning advanced supernatural disciplines and combat skills that Dutch has no knowledge of, creating a complex, challenging endgame dynamic. |

# 7. Art Direction

## 7.1 Visual Tone

New Amsterdam occupies the visual and tonal space between Assassin's Creed's historical grandeur and the dread atmosphere of From Software's dark-world aesthetic. Each historical era has its own dominant color palette and lighting mood, while the game's overall identity is built on darkness punctuated by vivid, period-specific light sources.

| Act | Palette |
|-----|--------|
| **Act 1 — Colonial** | Earthy browns, candlelight golds, deep forest greens. The settlement is small, dirty, and lit mostly by fire. The forest looms. Nights are genuinely dark. |
| **Act 2 — Revolution** | Gunpowder grey and deep red. The city is burning in places. The visual language of the era is chaos barely contained — buildings half-built, streets half-controlled. |
| **Act 3 — Gilded Age** | The gaslamp era: amber and shadow. Opulence in specific neighborhoods contrasted with grinding poverty in others. The city becomes vertical — the eye is drawn upward. |
| **Act 4 — Prohibition** | Film noir palette. Deep shadow, slants of harsh artificial light, the blue-grey of cigarette smoke and jazz clubs. Neon begins to appear late in this Act. |
| **Act 5 — Modern** | The full sensory overload of contemporary Manhattan — light pollution, screens, advertising, the compressed energy of millions of lives — seen through the eyes of something that remembers when it was all forest. |

## 7.2 Dutch's Visual Design

Dutch's appearance should convey centuries of adaptation rather than arrested development. He dresses in the style of each era — not perfectly, but well enough to pass. His eyes carry something that careful NPCs notice and cannot name. His movement is slightly wrong — too fluid, too precise, the product of four centuries of physical mastery.

## 7.3 Camera & Perspective

Third-person camera, close to Dutch's body during ground-level navigation to keep the world large and threatening. Pulls back significantly during rooftop traversal to emphasize verticality and speed. Combat uses a dynamic camera that follows the action closely, making Dutch's overwhelming physical advantage viscerally clear.

# 8. Audio Direction

## 8.1 Musical Identity

The score of New Amsterdam evolves with the game's historical setting. Each Act has its own musical language drawn from the genuine musical traditions of that era, arranged through a consistent orchestral framework that gives the game a cohesive sonic identity across its full timeline.

| Act | Sound |
|-----|-------|
| **Act 1 — Colonial** | Simple, sparse. Fife, lute, early keyboard instruments. The music of a small, uncertain settlement. |
| **Act 2 — Revolution** | Military percussion. Fife and drum. Tension built through sparse instrumentation suddenly overwhelmed by battlefield noise. |
| **Act 3 — Gilded Age** | Full orchestra. The confidence of a city that believes it is the center of the world. Classical composition with industrial rhythmic undertones. |
| **Act 4 — Prohibition** | Jazz. Big band. The score becomes syncopated, unpredictable, alive. The danger in the music matches the danger in the streets. |
| **Act 5 — Modern** | Electronic elements emerge alongside the orchestral and jazz foundations. The complete sound of 400 years of musical evolution, layered. |

## 8.2 Ambient Sound Design

The ambient sound of Manhattan must evolve as completely as the visual world. From the creak of colonial timber and the distant sound of forest and water, to the compressed roar of modern New York — traffic, construction, sirens, 8 million lives generating noise — the sonic world of each era must be immediately recognizable.

Dutch's vampire senses create a unique audio design challenge and opportunity: at full hunger, his enhanced hearing can be toggled to reveal information about nearby humans — heartbeats, whispered conversations, the specific sound of blood moving. This is both a navigation tool and a source of dread.

# 9. Technical Considerations

## 9.1 Target Platform Specifications

| Spec | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Primary Platforms | PlayStation 5, Xbox Series X\|S |
| Resolution Target | 4K / 60fps (Performance Mode), 4K / 30fps (Quality Mode) |
| World Scale | Full Manhattan Island — approximately 13.4 miles long, 2.3 miles wide at maximum |
| Storage Estimate | TBD — significant due to multi-era asset sets for the same geographic locations |
| Ray Tracing | Targeted for lighting and reflection systems, particularly critical for the period-specific lighting design |

## 9.2 Key Technical Challenges

- **Era Transition System**: The same physical geography must exist in multiple historical states simultaneously, with seamless transitions between states. This is the game's largest technical challenge.
- **NPC Density Scaling**: Population density increases dramatically across Acts. Modern-Manhattan NPC counts will require sophisticated crowd simulation systems.
- **Procedural Aging**: Buildings, streets, and infrastructure must visually age and change between Acts. Some transitions must be observable by the player mid-game.
- **Daylight System**: A precise, unforgiving daylight simulation that tracks sun position by season and time, creating genuine urgency around Dutch's daily survival routine.

# 10. Monetization & Business Model

## 10.1 Model

New Amsterdam is designed as a premium, full-price release ($69.99 USD) with no microtransactions, loot boxes, or pay-to-win mechanics.

## 10.2 Post-Launch Content

- **Expansion Model**: Each historical era is modular enough that additional sub-eras can be inserted between Acts (e.g., The Civil War, The Great Depression) as full-price DLC packs.
- **No live-service**: This is a single-player, narrative-driven experience. Post-launch support focuses on expansions, not seasonal content.
